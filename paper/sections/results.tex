\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Main Comparison}
\label{sec:main_comparison}

We evaluate LLM agents on the schedule-only interface with a
5-hour wall-clock budget and the same fixed skeleton. The 500-multi-start
SGD baseline achieves 4246.7\,GWh on the
held-out ROWP farm. We test two frontier models (Claude Code, Gemini
2.5 Flash) and one self-hosted open-source model (DeepSeek R1
Distill 32B, run on LUMI via vLLM with the portable scaffold).
Figure~\ref{fig:rowp} shows the best held-out AEP for each method.

\textbf{The two frontier LLMs cluster at the same local optimum} on the
held-out farm. In schedule-only mode, Claude Code's best feasible
schedule reaches 4271.5\,GWh (+24.8) and Gemini CLI's reaches
4269.3\,GWh (+22.6); Gemini CLI's best overall result (full-optimizer
mode, best of five seeds) reaches 4272.7\,GWh (+26.0). All three
fall within seed noise of the bump-family DE upper bound
(4272.9\,GWh) and within $\sim$4\,GWh of random search at matched
budget (4268.6\,GWh)---a tight cluster at the 4272.7\,GWh
shared local optimum.

\textbf{The open-source model does not reach this optimum.}
DeepSeek R1 Distill 32B, self-hosted on LUMI via vLLM with the
same portable scaffold, achieves 5537.6\,GWh on training ($-3.1$
below baseline) in an iterative seed tournament (4 seeds $\times$
3 generations). Despite producing feasible layouts on all 48
evaluation cells, its best schedule consistently underperforms the
baseline (Fig.~\ref{fig:matrix}, green). This capability gap
persists across both scaffold versions (v1 and v2 with updated
memory template), confirming it reflects model capability rather
than scaffold design.

\subsection{Training vs Held-Out Divergence}
\label{sec:divergence}

\textbf{The training-optimal schedule is not the held-out-optimal
schedule, and infeasibility is the dominant failure mode.} Several
agents produced schedules with training AEP at the practical
ceiling (5600.0\,GWh) whose \emph{nominal} ROWP AEP exceeded the
true held-out winners---but these layouts violate boundary or
spacing constraints. After filtering out infeasible ROWP layouts,
Claude's best schedule scores 4271.5\,GWh and Gemini's best
schedule-only result scores 4269.3\,GWh (Table~\ref{tab:main});
Gemini's best across modes is 4272.7\,GWh from a full-optimizer
seed (training AEP 5542.2, iter 27; Appendix~\ref{app:fullopt}).
Training AEP at the ceiling does not distinguish feasible from
infeasible generalizers.

\textbf{Training AEP has a ceiling near 5600\,GWh.} Both frontier
models hit 5600.0 exactly on multiple attempts, suggesting the
training landscape has a practically reachable local optimum.

\paragraph{Deployment selection.}
A practitioner without a validation farm would deploy the schedule
with the highest training AEP among those passing unit tests.
We compare this \emph{deploy} selection against an \emph{oracle}
that picks the best feasible held-out AEP
(Fig.~\ref{fig:deploy_oracle}).  For Claude, the deploy script
(iter~179, train=5566.6) differs from the oracle (iter~192,
train=5555.7); on ROWP the oracle gains 8.1\,GWh.  For Gemini,
deploy and oracle select the same script (iter~118).  The
deployment gap is small across all 48 cells
(Fig.~\ref{fig:deploy_oracle}), confirming that the unit-test
suite is a reliable proxy for held-out feasibility.

Training AEP saturates within 1--2 hours for the frontier
models but the best feasible held-out AEP continues to improve
throughout the 5-hour budget (Fig.~\ref{fig:convergence_holdout});
a time budget that terminates when training AEP plateaus would
miss late-run improvements in generalization
(Appendix~\ref{app:running_best}, Fig.~\ref{fig:running}).

\subsection{Generalization Across the 48-Cell Matrix}
\label{sec:generalization}

A schedule discovered on one farm configuration should generalize
across turbine counts, wind resources, and polygon geometries.
To test this, we construct a $2 \times 6 \times 4 = 48$-cell
evaluation matrix crossing two farm polygons, six turbine counts,
and four wind roses (Table~\ref{tab:matrix}).

500-multi-start baseline.  Key findings:

\paragraph{Gains increase with packing density.}
At low $N$ (30--40), the polygon is spacious and the baseline
already near-optimal; LLM schedules offer $<\!5\%$ improvement.
At high $N$ (70--80), spacing constraints bind and the baseline's
feasibility rate drops below 30\% on the tightest cells
(Table~\ref{tab:matrix_feas}).  LLM schedules maintain feasibility
and achieve 20--40\% gains over baseline, suggesting the learned
penalty dynamics handle constraint pressure better than the fixed
$\alpha \propto 1/\eta$ ramp.

\paragraph{Generalization to the held-out ROWP polygon.}
The ROWP polygon (bottom row of Fig.~\ref{fig:matrix}) shows the
same trend as DEI despite the schedule never seeing ROWP during
training. This is positive transfer on a single held-out polygon
($N=1$ held-out geometry); broader cross-geometry claims would
require additional polygons drawn from independent offshore cases.

\paragraph{Wind rose sensitivity.}
The realistic multi-directional roses (DEI, ROWP, omnidirectional)
show consistent positive transfer: schedules trained on the DEI
rose generalize to the ROWP rose without degradation.  However,
the uniform single-direction rose (column~1 of Fig.~\ref{fig:matrix})
is a constructive failure case: LLM schedules score at or below the
baseline.  With a single wind direction, the optimal layout
degenerates to a 1D row perpendicular to the wind---a qualitatively
different landscape from the 2D spreading required by
multi-directional roses.  The penalty dynamics discovered on the
DEI rose (Gaussian bumps, penalty coupling) are tuned for 2D
exploration and do not transfer to this degenerate 1D geometry.

\paragraph{Generalization boundary.}
This failure sharpens the generalization claim: the agent
discovered penalty dynamics for 2D constrained layout
optimization, not a universal optimization schedule. Within this
DEI$\to$ROWP train/test pair, schedules transfer across turbine
count ($N\!=\!30$ to $N\!=\!80$) and realistic multi-directional
wind resources, but not to qualitatively different optimization
landscapes. Transfer to additional polygon geometries beyond ROWP
is untested. The single-cell training protocol makes this
boundary precise and falsifiable on every held-out cell
individually.

\paragraph{Cross-seed variance and mode comparison.}
We run Gemini 2.5 Flash in both modes: five independent seeds in
full-optimizer mode (Gemini API, 5-hour budget each) and one seed
in schedule-only mode (Gemini CLI).
Figure~\ref{fig:mode_comparison} separates the results by mode.

In full-optimizer mode (left panel), best feasible ROWP AEP varies
from +4.2 (no hot-start) to +26.0 (Seed~2) GWh over baseline
across five seeds---a 6$\times$ range.  A single run gives a noisy
estimate of the method's capability.  With the corrected system
prompt (which prohibits file I/O and requires the function to
use its arguments), a fresh 2-hour Gemini run produced a
SLSQP-based optimizer with JAX Jacobians that achieves
5557.1\,GWh on training (+16.4) and generalizes across the
48-cell matrix (Fig.~\ref{fig:mode_comparison}, right, dashed).

The right panel shows the generalization curves. The SLSQP
full-optimizer (dashed) is the strongest on the training polygon
at high $N$, consistent with SLSQP being the established
method in the wind farm community. Schedule-only scripts
(solid lines) produce more consistent cross-seed results and
generalize well, despite being constrained to the less-standard
SGD skeleton. DeepSeek R1 32B consistently underperforms
regardless of mode.

\subsection{Discovered Schedule Structures}
\label{sec:structures}

The top frontier-LLM schedules use three qualitatively different
mechanisms:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Claude iter 192}: \emph{dual Gaussian LR bumps} at
  $t=0.5, 0.75$ with a coordinated penalty dip at $t=0.6$.
\item \textbf{Gemini iter 118}: \emph{8-cycle cosine restarts}
  (7 short + 1 long) with cyclic Adam betas, aggressive $\alpha
  \propto t^3$ penalty ramp, and a ``squeeze'' phase at $t>0.98$
  where $\alpha$ jumps to $5\!\times\!10^6 \alpha_0$.
\item \textbf{DeepSeek iter 004}: \emph{constant-then-cosine}
  LR decay with $\alpha \propto 1/\eta$ coupling and standard
  Adam ($\beta_1\!=\!0.9$, $\beta_2\!=\!0.999$).
\end{itemize}
All three maintain high penalty weight $\alpha$ at convergence.
A controlled ablation (Sec.~\ref{sec:discussion}) shows that
penalty scale affects held-out AEP quality ($\pm$11\,GWh) but
not feasibility; the LR dynamics themselves are sufficient for
feasible held-out layouts.  The frontier schedules (Claude, Gemini)
introduce non-monotonic LR dynamics (bumps, restarts) that the
open-source model does not discover.
Detailed parameter plots and per-mechanism intuition are in
Appendix~\ref{app:structures}.

\subsection{Random-Search and Within-Family Ablations}
\label{sec:ablations}

To isolate the LLM's contribution from the richness of the
parameterized schedule family itself, we run two non-LLM ablations
with budgets matched to the LLM schedule-only runs. Both ablations
share the same skeleton, training farm, and held-out evaluation
protocol as the LLM agents.

\paragraph{Random search over a parameterized family.}
We sample $N=320$ schedules (matching Claude's attempt count) from
a parameterized family covering cosine / exponential / linear /
polynomial LR decay with optional warmup, optional sinusoidal shake
or Gaussian bumps, monotonic penalty ramps (linear / quadratic /
coupled), and random Adam betas. \emph{This family was designed
after inspecting the LLM-generated schedules}: it includes Gaussian
bumps and sinusoidal perturbations because the LLMs proposed those
structures. The ablation therefore tests whether the
LLM-discovered structures are simple enough to be found by random
search within a known family, not whether random search would have
discovered these structures independently.
Each sample is scored on DEI
(training) and ROWP (held-out) with the same feasibility check.
\textbf{Result:} of 320 samples, 163 produced feasible ROWP layouts;
the best feasible random-search schedule reaches \textbf{4268.6\,GWh}
on the held-out farm---within $\sim$4\,GWh of both LLM frontier
schedules (Claude 4271.5, Gemini 4272.7) and within seed noise of
the bump-family DE result (4272.9).

\paragraph{Differential evolution within Claude's bump family.}
We parameterize Claude's iter 192 dual-bump structure as a
14-dim family (LR, warmup, two bump amplitudes, centers, widths,
penalty coupling, quadratic boost, $\beta_1$, $\beta_2$) and run
scipy DE (\texttt{popsize=15}, \texttt{maxiter=20}, 316 evals) in
two conditions: \textbf{Claude-seeded} (iter 192's params) reaches
\textbf{4272.9\,GWh}; \textbf{baseline-seeded} (zero bumps,
TopFarm $\beta_1=0.1$, $\beta_2=0.2$) reaches \textbf{4271.9\,GWh}.
The two runs agree within seed noise: the bump-family
parameterization reaches the 4272.7\,GWh optimum without the LLM's
structural hint, so Claude's specific bump placement is not
load-bearing once the parameterization exists.

\paragraph{Substrate-independent tiebreaker: CMA-ES on raw 148-D.}
All methods above share pixwake physics and (for four of five) an
Adam-plus-penalty continuous-relaxation skeleton. To test whether
the 4272.7\,GWh cluster is a real local optimum or a shared-substrate
fixed point, we run CMA-ES directly on the raw $2N=148$-dimensional
position vector with no Adam, no schedule, no penalty ramp, no JAX
gradients, and no polish---feasibility enforced as a continuous
soft penalty. \textbf{Cold-start} from a feasible wind-aware grid
init (4039.6\,GWh): at a 4500-evaluation compute cap (comparable
to the LLM schedule-only budgets of 254--320 evaluations and the
bump-family DE budget of 316), CMA-ES reaches \textbf{4189.3},
still climbing but $\sim$80\,GWh below the cluster. This gap
reflects compute-starvation at the matched budget rather than
substrate failure: the direct 148-dimensional search simply needs
much more compute to climb from a generic init.
\textbf{Warm-start} from Claude iter 192's converged layout
(4271.5): 6000 evals with 68\% feasibility rate reach only
\textbf{4272.2}, an improvement of $+0.7$\,GWh---essentially
nothing. Substrate-independent local search cannot reach the
cluster from a generic init at matched compute \emph{and} cannot
meaningfully exceed it once warm-started inside. The 4272.7\,GWh
optimum is a real feature of the feasibility-filtered
landscape, not a shared-substrate artifact.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
Six search strategies with qualitatively different inductive
biases all cluster within $\sim$4\,GWh (Table~\ref{tab:main}), and
CMA-ES confirms the cluster is not a substrate fixed point. This
establishes a \textbf{shared local optimum at 4272.7\,GWh} for
this DEI$\to$ROWP pair. CMA-ES warm-started at Claude's layout
improves by only $+0.7$\,GWh in 6000 evaluations, confirming
this is a real landscape feature; cold-started CMA-ES reaches
only 4189.3 at a matched budget, reflecting compute starvation
in 148 dimensions rather than a substrate limitation---we cannot
rule out higher optima reachable by longer runs.
The LLM contribution is therefore
\emph{not} a score advantage---random search, baseline-init DE,
and warm-start CMA-ES all match within noise. The LLM contribution
is \textbf{parameterization discovery}: Claude proposed the
14-parameter dual-bump family (baseline-init DE still reaches
the optimum within it), and Gemini proposed both the
sinusoidal-shake schedule and a 6-parameter 2D lattice family
(via scipy DE + vectorized JAX; Sec.~\ref{sec:structures}) that
reaches the optimum through an unrelated route. The benchmark
measures which parameterizations LLMs propose and whether they
are rich enough to contain the optimum; absolute scores are
matched by non-LLM search at equivalent compute.


\paragraph{Full-optimizer mode.}
In full-optimizer mode, Gemini independently converges on
SLSQP~\cite{kraft1988software} with JAX-computed constraint
Jacobians---the standard approach in the wind farm optimization
community (e.g.,
TopFarm~\cite{pedersen2019topfarm})---rather than the gradient
descent skeleton used in schedule-only mode. This is a validation
of the agent's engineering judgment but does not produce new
optimization knowledge: SLSQP with exact boundary and spacing
Jacobians is well-established practice.

The schedule-only interface, by constraining the agent to the
less-explored SGD skeleton, forces it into unfamiliar territory
where it discovers genuinely novel schedule structures (Gaussian
bumps, cyclic restarts, terminal-alpha squeeze) that have no
precedent in the learning-rate schedule literature. The constraint
\emph{is} the contribution---mirroring the FunSearch finding that
restricting the search space to a narrow function interface drives
creative exploration~\cite{fawzi2024funsearch}.

Across five full-optimizer seeds
(Fig.~\ref{fig:mode_comparison}, left), the best seed
(+26.0\,GWh ROWP) matches the schedule-only result, but the median
(+17.3) and worst (+4.2) are substantially lower. Schedule-only
produces more consistent results and---crucially---scripts that
generalize across the 48-cell matrix without modification, since
they receive only \texttt{(step, total\_steps, lr0, alpha0)}.
