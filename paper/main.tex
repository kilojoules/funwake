\begin{abstract}
We introduce FunWake, a low-cost benchmark for evaluating whether
LLM agents can discover novel optimizer schedules. The task is
minimal: write a Python function returning four scalars (learning
rate, penalty weight, two Adam betas) at each step of a fixed
gradient optimizer for wind farm layout. A physics-based held-out
farm---with different turbine model, polygon, and wind
resource---catches overfitting that same-distribution evaluation
misses. Two frontier agents (Claude Code, Gemini CLI) discover
structurally distinct schedules (dual Gaussian bumps, sinusoidal
shake) that generalize across turbine counts $N\!=\!30$--$80$ and
exceed a 500-multi-start baseline by up to 0.6\%. Six search
strategies converge to a shared local optimum at 4272.7\,GWh
($+26$ over baseline), confirmed by substrate-independent CMA-ES.
The LLM contribution is \emph{parameterization discovery}: random
search and differential evolution within the LLM-proposed
schedule families reach the same optimum at matched compute.
FunWake is open-source, evaluates on CPU in 30--60\,s, and costs
\$5--50 per model.
\end{abstract}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

FunWake is a low-cost, open-source benchmark for LLM-discovered
optimizer schedules. The task is simple---write a 4-output Python
function---and evaluation is fast (30--60~seconds on CPU). Two findings
justify the benchmark's design: (1)~LLM agents discover genuinely novel
schedule structures (decaying sinusoidal perturbation, dual Gaussian
bumps) that generalize across farm sizes the agent never trained on, and
(2)~physics-based held-out evaluation is essential, as training AEP
improvements of +9~GWh can correspond to held-out degradation.

The held-out farm surfaces a shared local optimum at
4272.7\,GWh that six structurally distinct search strategies all
reach from different starting points and different
parameterizations; substrate-independent CMA-ES warm-started at
this optimum improves by only $+0.7$\,GWh in 6000 samples,
confirming it is a real landscape feature rather than a substrate
artifact.
The LLM contribution is parameterization discovery: Claude
proposed the 14-parameter dual-bump family, within which
baseline-seeded DE (zero bumps, no LLM structural hint) still
reaches the same optimum; Gemini proposed the sinusoidal-shake schedule
and a separate 6-parameter 2D lattice family that reaches the
optimum through an unrelated route. The structures are rich enough
to contain the optimum; non-LLM search inside them matches the
LLM-deployed schedules at matched compute. We release all 700+
generated schedules, the evaluation harness, and seven farm
variants at \url{https://github.com/kilojoules/funwake}.

\section{Discovered Schedule Structures (Detailed)}
\label{app:structures}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figs/fig3_top3_schedules.png}
\caption{Schedule parameters over optimization progress for the
baseline (TopFarm-style monotonic), Claude's iter 192 (dual Gaussian
LR bumps at $t=0.5, 0.75$ with penalty dip at $t=0.6$), and Gemini's
iter 067 (80-cycle sinusoidal shake with $\sqrt{1-t}$ amplitude
decay; $\beta_1=0$; quadratic alpha ramp to $7500\times$). Note the
log-scale $\alpha$ axis: Gemini's penalty weight reaches two orders
of magnitude higher than the baseline.}
\label{fig:schedules}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Claude's dual Gaussian bumps.}
The learning rate is $4\eta_0 \cdot \texttt{cosine}(t)$ with Gaussian
bumps at $t=0.5$ and $t=0.75$ ($+20\%$ and $+30\%$ of initial rate).
Penalty weight grows as $\alpha_\text{base}(t) \cdot (1 - 0.5 \cdot
\exp(-(t-0.6)^2/0.0032))$, with a dip at $t=0.6$ between the bumps.
$\beta_1 = 0.3$, $\beta_2 = 0.5$. The intuition: bumps escape local
optima, the penalty dip permits rearrangement during the second
bump, and moderate momentum damps post-bump oscillations.

\paragraph{Gemini's sinusoidal shake.}
The learning rate is $\eta_\text{cosine}(t) \cdot (1 + 0.08\sqrt{1-t}
\cdot \sin(80\pi t))$---a high-frequency perturbation (80 cycles
over the run) with $\sqrt{1-t}$ amplitude decay. The penalty weight
is $\alpha_0 (1 + 7499 t^2)$, reaching $7500\times$ by the final
iteration (the baseline reaches $\sim$100$\times$). $\beta_1 = 0$
(no momentum). The intuition: continuous shake prevents the
optimizer from settling into a local minimum early; extreme
late-stage penalty forces feasibility regardless of how the
schedule approaches convergence.

\paragraph{Penalty weight affects quality, not feasibility.}
A controlled ablation multiplying Claude iter~192's $\alpha$ by
factors 0.01$\times$--10$\times$ while holding LR fixed shows all
nine variants produce feasible ROWP layouts. Held-out AEP peaks at
the original scale (4271.5\,GWh) and degrades symmetrically at both
extremes ($-$8--11\,GWh), suggesting the LR dynamics (bumps,
cosine decay) are sufficient for feasibility; the penalty trajectory
controls AEP quality on the held-out farm.

\section{Running-Best AEP Over Time}
\label{app:running_best}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{figs/fig4_running_best.png}
\caption{Running-best training AEP (top) and running-best feasible
held-out (ROWP) AEP (bottom) over wall-clock time. Stars mark the
final \emph{deployed} script for each model---the one with the
highest feasible ROWP at run's end. Training AEP saturates quickly
for frontier models; feasible ROWP continues to improve throughout
the 5-hour budget.}
\label{fig:running}
\end{figure}

Training AEP saturates within $\sim$1--2 hours for the frontier
models, but the best-feasible held-out AEP continues to improve
throughout the 5-hour budget. This matters for benchmark design: a
time budget that terminates when training AEP plateaus would miss
late-run improvements in generalization.

\section{Full-Optimizer Mode}
\label{app:fullopt}

For completeness, we also run the two frontier models in
full-optimizer mode (writing an arbitrary \texttt{optimize()}
function). Both converge to the same strategy: wrap
\texttt{topfarm\_sgd\_solve} with a better initialization
(hexagonal lattice, K-means clustering, wind-direction-aware
placement) and tuned hyperparameters. Neither produces a custom
gradient optimizer that passes held-out feasibility.
Full-optimizer held-out AEP: Claude 4268.5 (+21.8); Gemini 4264.0
(+17.3). The schedule-only interface produces marginally better
held-out results for both, suggesting that constraining the search
space forces deeper exploration of optimization dynamics.
